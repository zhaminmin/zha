Abstract

In the E-Laas project, a novel and operational energy-based approach to designing multimodal urban logistics systems is proposed. As such, urban delivery systems are compared by their energy used, including modal shift (micro platforms) and novel ways of combining charging and freight parking. This energy-based quantitative methodology enables us to systematically and naturally define sustainability aims. Thus, our overall aim with E-Laas is to create a novel energy minimizing method from consolidation centers to the customers' doors incentivizing energy savings at each level. Furthermore, the energy-based methodology enables us to contrast different modular solutions via the concept of interlaced energy footprinting.

The aims of the project are:

  • A1: representing urban logistics missions as logistics flows.
  • A2: defining operational (and complementary) energy use in logistics flows.
  • A3: analyzing spatio-temporal energy usage in urban logistics missions and perform sensitivity analysis (grid interruption, scalability).
  • A4: energy awareness on multiple levels (from customers to asuthorities) is aimed to incentivize.

Assumptions, Project Objectives and Targets

Assumptions

♠ AP1: We assume that the flow of goods related to the development of civilization together with consumption will not cease to increase. If no coordinated actions are done, it will negatively impact our lives.
♠ AP2: The development of digital and telecommunications techniques makes it possible to implement IoT, ICT, and ITS solutions in cities. It is hence assumed that relevant operational, and real-time data are available.
♠ AP3: The development of synchronized, energy-optimal operational actions is assumed to enhance sustainability, efficiency, and social awareness of city logistics since we leverage optimization methods to improve the systems. As such, we also assume that the problems of urban logistics operations can be explicitly formulated as optimization problems, for which some modelling assumptions will be needed.
♠ AP4: It is assumed that increased environmental awareness of residents and service providers related to the implementation of new solutions will positively impact the sustainable transition.
♠ AP5: Calibration data to emulate service aspects behind E-Laas is provided by the partners/publicly available. Customers behave rationally.
♠ AP6: Socio aspects can be interpreted within/map to the energy aware methodology.
♠ AP7: We assume that cities will have an overview, and can use E-Laas to create legal standards to incentivise resource optimal urban shipping.
♠ AP8: Solution and technological tools to make the impact via the E-Laas methods exist and are operational.

Objectives

♠ O1: contributing to sustainable developments of cities and communities.
♠ O2: creating resilient logistics methods, digital and physical infrastructure for sustainable transport solutions.
♠ O3: developing sustainable and climate-responsive urban logistic systems.

Targets

♠ T1: Develop energy-optimal urban logistics solutions (from consolidation centers to doors).
♠ T2: Develop smart multimodal and energy optimizing micro delivery systems.
♠ T3: Analyze logistics mission interruptions and advise with reconfiguration solutions to regain energy optimality.
♠ T4: Couple delivery platforms and unloading zones via charging facility to the power grid.
♠ T5: Quantify demand for freight and analyze the customer behavior change required to run the project E-Laas.
♠ T6: Scalability of multimodal and energy optimal distribution system.
♠ T7: Devise new ways to enhance societal responsibility by E-Laas.

Key Activities and Work Programme Description

Overall strategy for the work plan

We focus on teamwork, and all work packages are done by multiple partners. Four universities as the main partners will be more active in the project to undertake the core research work. However, other cooperation partners, including public sectors and companies, will be less work loading responsible for the collaborative work and undertaking the auxiliary work of providing data, pilot implementation, policy support, etc.

The project is composed of 7 work packages combined into a logical workflow.

WP0 is the starting task of the project. It develops detailed assumptions for the implementation of further works. The realization of WPs 1, 2, 3, and 4 is parallel. WP1 is independent of WP3 and WP4, which are closely related according to the implementation methodology. WP2 will be implemented on the base of assumptions from WP0 and will be fed with the results of WPs 1, 3, and 4, influencing the final result. WP2 and WP3 contain simulation studies, but at a different level of detail, it should be emphasized that research in WP2 is of fundamental nature to gain new knowledge, and in WP3, applied research to verify and validate the developed mathematical models and algorithms. WP5 is aimed at consolidating the project results in the assumed impact path and will also consider the effects of analyses from WP2. The WP6 aims to ensure unrestricted and broad access to the project's results, implementation progress, and the distribution of its outcomes among stakeholders.

Relevance and Impact Pathway

Relevance — Contribution of the project to the aims and expectations of the call

E-Laas proposes a socio-economic solution to reach the holistic goal with sustainable urban logistics well exploiting the benefits of digital and physical infrastructure. In E-Laas, an overall energy footprint is minimized and coupled to the power grid. Furthermore, the societal responsibility in achieving such a transition is emphasized via environmental, ethical, and eco-philanthropic aspects. We cover the following topics in the call from Theme 1.

♣ We develop energy-optimal urban logistics solutions from distribution centers to customers' doors.
♣ We create smart multimodal and energy-optimizing micro delivery systems.
♣ We provide tools to analyze logistics mission interruptions and propose reconfiguration solutions.
♣ We couple delivery platforms and unloading zones via the charging facility to the power grid for more efficient recharging.
♣ We quantify the demand and customer perceptions that drive E-Laas.
♣ We scale multimodal and energy-optimal distribution systems for different cities.

Strategic Activity Planning

Stakeholder engagement

E-Laas involves a balanced team comprised of: (a) universities, (b) authorities at different levels, (c) industry actors.

E-Laas will focus mainly on three types of actors that are part of business and society:
(i) society and public sector have a clean environment and a fossil-free transport system as a priority for the next 30 years, which means city authorities need to get knowledge on how to transition towards a new e-system and how to satisfy business and society needs to make this new reality feasible;
(ii) transport companies that are in the middle of a transition towards e-vehicles and need a new system designed for this transition that ensures a reliable supply of energy at the right place and time;
(iii) tech-based companies and innovators, these are companies with innovative business models that aim at creating value from digitalization and the need for a new system towards electrification. The models and simulation will use the input from these actors.

Communication

Communication and dissemination strategy: In order to ensure that the results of E-Laas will have an extensive reach during the project but also the desired success after the project, a strategy will be designed to raise awareness of the project’s results. Project partners will integrate the project communication with their own well-established communication networks.

E-Laas will use a combination of communication channels, including:

♦ Digital communication and social media: this will offer an immediate form of communication.
♦ Conferences and events: key conferences and events including (not limited to): TRA Transport Research Arena, the VREF Conference on Urban Freight, INFORMS Annual Meeting, and TRB annual meetings.
♦ Project events: there will be at least three local stakeholder workshops to engage local and national stakeholders and present them with the project results.
♦ Scientific publications: E-Laas will publish scientific papers in peer-reviewed journals: e.g. Transportation Research Part series. We aim at publishing in open access venues without restriction in order to enlarge the target audience.

Monitoring, Evaluation and Learning

The E-Laas project will follow the commonly known Plan, Do, Check and Act circle, and will start by identifying the needs of the main stakeholders. Based on these needs, E-Laas will develop and implement an energy-based framework for urban logistics systems and new innovative services.

Based on real-life experiences and stated expectations and perceptions E-Laas will assess the actual impacts. Measured impacts and perceived benefits will be used in the implementation guidelines. The impacts of the proposed solutions will be measured by well-defined and measurable Key Performance Indicators (KPIs). Some of the key elements in order to monitor the process of the project are: International cooperation: The success of the project will require cooperation and alignment of the work in the different work packages and cities and countries involved. Cross-fertilising operation between stakeholders: The lessons learned will be shared between the stakeholders, thus each stakeholder will not only gain experiences from their own work but from the work of all of the other partners in all cities. This will be done on multiple levels, from frequent management meetings to webinars. Close monitoring of operational KPIs: Project management will ensure closer monitoring or agreed actions and operational KPIs.

Capacity strengthening

This project will use two pillars to support the capacity strengthening strategy in a way that results are both cutting-edge research and ready for utilization and scaling-up: (i) the academic network and (ii) the engagement of stakeholders and their networks.

Consortium collaboration

Cooperation partners: The project involves many cooperation partners from both sides (Sino-European). Partners representing authorities, SE, and LE cover a wide range of responsibilities both in terms of activities and size.

Synergies within Group Europe: CUT will dedicate to the tasks of the business models designed for E-Laas. These tasks first need sufficient data and information to support the designs and also need the simulation analysis to provide feedback to improve the design. On that front, data collection and simulation analysis are the strengths of WUT.

Synergies within Group China:SU will dedicate to planning large-scale urban logistics systems for E-Laas. These strategic planning decisions have strong independencies with operations. To complement that, TU will dedicate to designing operational models and algorithms for the systems, which will provide feedback to strategic planning.

Synergies between Group China and Group Europe:Group Europe will build the concepts and foundation of E-Laas. And based on that, Group China will push them to the deployment and implementation. However, this is not unidirectional. The deployment and implementation will generate more data and knowledge for us to better understand E-Laas, and based on that we can re-design the concepts and foundation.

Diversity aspects (including gender dimension) or specific populations' consideration

The diversity of this project reflects multiple aspects. First, the selection of our team partners is considered from multiple perspectives. Second, the choice of each member is also prudent, considering the multiple dimension diversity. Third, our research covers most logistics issues across the energy-based multimodal urban logistics system.

Added value of Sino-European cooperation

— The variety of Chinese and European cities in terms of different population sizes, urban layouts, geographical conditions, etc., is an opportunity for an in-depth understanding of the processes taking place and for matching solution modules to the scale of the system.
— Communities, companies, and individuals in Europe have made significant achievements in sustainability. Thus, Sino-European cooperation can lead to advanced sustainable logistics solutions.
— The Chinese market is very dynamic in the development of micro delivery systems. This creates great industrial opportunities to deploy E-Laas solutions.
— The characteristics of customers, their different behaviors, and their needs in the Chinese and European markets will enable a thorough examination of a wide range of factors influencing the demand for specific services and allow the precise adjustment of the E-Laas architecture.
— Sharing good experiences and practices between partners, cultural enrichment.
— Strengthening relations between selected European and Chinese cities forming the consortium.

Management structure and procedures

The overall project management of E-Laas will comply with the following two major principles: (1) Principle of creating an integrated project structure incorporating technical, scientific and partner coordination; and (2) Principle of achieving agreements upon all partners guaranteeing the arrangement of decision making, and concealing the reliable agreements to protect intellectual properties of all partners. Procedures include communication amongst consortium partners, consortium meetings, decision-making process, conflict resolution procedure, conflict resolution procedure and monitoring and reporting.

Ethical and Regulatory Considerations

Data management practices will follow the EU Code for the Responsible Conduct of Research. All data collection and processing tasks within E-Laas consortium will be GDPR compliant. Any collected data such as urban infrastructure data and survey data, will be anonymized/pseudonymized. Partners must explain how all the data they intend to process is relevant and limited to the purposes of their intended task in accordance with the “data minimization” principle.

Data Management

E-Laas has the following Data Management Plan (DMP) following the open-source publishing policy where it is possible.

Storage, archive. Data storage and backup of research data during the project will be stored at the partners and at the E-Laas website. After the end of the E-Laas (during), partners publication libraries will be used. Some data will be published together with papers.

Data type. Type 1: Results will be based on synthetic data in WP2, WP3, WP4. Type 2: Confidential data in WP0 or WP1, not shared. Type 3: Data generated via questionary in WP2 will be anonymized and shared.

Standards. We will use available standards when working with data: ISO/IEC JTC1/SC32. Type 1 data will use SI units when synthetically created for WP2-4. Type 2 data in WP0-1 uses SI units (duration, position).

Documentation. In WP0-4, we will clearly describe how the data were generated or collected/received.

Compliance with legislation, ethical requirements. No individual or personal data will be used in the project. Chinese and European (GDPR) legislation will be enforced, whichever is more restrictive.

Privacy, confidentiality, security, intellectual property, or other rights or requirements. The consortium partners will lay down all issues linked to access rights, protection of IP, the use of licenses, etc., in the Consortium Agreement, which will be concluded at the start of the project.

Re-use, re-distribution, and the production of derivatives. When publishing with Type 1 and partially Type 3 data in E-Laas we follow CC BY-NC.

Resources. Each partner is responsible to run their dedicated data management and ensuring that data will be FAIR.
